This is my first time writing a retrospective. Since it's a personal piece, I'll keep the tone casual.

![My fairly green 2022 contribution graph](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67324487/210123005-de6e74c4-437b-4480-a43f-e49bd1e96dea.png)
`My fairly green 2022 contribution graph`

Honestly, 2022 was a less packed year than the one before. 2021 was when I first started learning to code — I jumped from a government-funded bootcamp straight into the Wanted pre-onboarding course, a reboot camp, and nonstop team projects. This year, aside from a three-month frontend development internship at a startup early on, I wasn't part of any formal program or workplace. So I'll confess: as I sit down to write this, it's hard to point to specific things and say "I did that." But that's exactly why I need to look back and take stock. I want to gather the scattered traces, records, and memories from this year so the version of me who worked hard all year long won't feel forgotten.

## January – March: Making Money with Code

I'd worked all kinds of part-time jobs since I was twenty, but this was the first time I earned money from code I wrote. It was also my first time working at a company, so I was clumsy in many ways. I'm grateful to my colleagues for being so understanding.

### Documentation and Comments for the Future

I've already written about my [internship retrospective](https://nvrtmd.github.io/%EC%9D%B8%ED%84%B4%EC%89%BD-%ED%94%84%EB%A1%A0%ED%8A%B8%EC%97%94%EB%93%9C-%EC%9D%B8%ED%84%B4%EC%89%BD-%ED%9A%8C%EA%B3%A0-%EB%AC%B8%EC%84%9C%ED%99%94-%EC%95%84%EC%B9%B4%EC%9D%B4%EB%B9%99/) in [other posts](https://medium.com/playkeyboard/%EC%9D%B8%ED%84%B4%EC%8B%AD-%EC%A0%80%EB%8A%94-%EB%8D%94-%EB%82%98%EC%9D%80-%EC%B5%9C%EC%84%A0%EC%9D%84-%EB%A7%8C%EB%93%A4%EA%B3%A0-%EC%8B%B6%EC%9D%80-%EA%B0%9C%EB%B0%9C%EC%9E%90-%EB%AF%BC%EC%9C%A0%EC%A7%80%EC%9E%85%EB%8B%88%EB%8B%A4-c37f44b08e14), so let me share some things that didn't make it into those. As the only frontend developer on the team, I did my best to build a solid structure on my own. That meant thorough documentation, as I wrote about on my blog, and a reasonable amount of code comments. The product has already left my hands, but I hope my efforts are at least somewhat useful to whoever took over the product I built from scratch.

### Failure, Then a Second Attempt

While building the product, I used redux-saga for the first time. About six months before my internship, the final assignment of the pre-onboarding course I'd taken required using redux-saga. Back then, even Redux itself was overwhelming for someone only six months into development, so I ended up submitting that assignment incomplete without properly touching redux-saga. Then my senior developer — a full-stack developer — suggested I try it out to modularize and systematically manage async communication. I was worried since I'd already failed once, but I said I'd give it a shot.

I spent nearly two to three days after work, staying up until dawn, cycling through endless Googling, video lectures, and writing code myself. Eventually, I managed to apply it successfully to the product. I won't belabor how proud and happy I felt when I confirmed it was actually working.

Looking back now, though, while it was a great opportunity for me to work with React, TypeScript, and redux-saga, the product wasn't that large. Structurally, we probably could have done without Redux entirely. I sometimes wonder if react-query (which I still haven't tried myself, but based on what I've read) would have been a better fit.

### Tough but Capable

On the other hand, choosing TypeScript was absolutely the right call. (Though I've been mostly using JavaScript again lately, so I need to sharpen those skills back up.) It was a great opportunity to properly use TypeScript. It feels difficult and tedious at first, but when I was cleaning up comments before leaving, I realized how much it helps with maintenance to have types defined for everything — from variables to state and props, no matter how small. Code that speaks for itself. TypeScript makes that happen.

### Leaving with Regrets

The latter half of the internship got hectic with deadlines piling up. The campaign page I built was scheduled to launch after my last day, so I left feeling like I hadn't fully finished my work. What I regret most is never getting the chance to do code reviews — there was no other frontend developer or dedicated frontend mentor on the team. It was a product filled entirely with my code, so I have both lingering regrets and deep attachment. I'm just grateful it's still running well to this day.

## April – May: Coding Test Study, Climbing a Big Mountain

If you look at the contribution graph above, April is pretty empty. But I was actually solving at least one coding test problem a day during that time. I accidentally planted those contributions on a different GitHub account, which is a shame.

![A small contribution graph on another GitHub account](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67324487/210123102-da8b59c5-c553-42e9-91cc-86dc746709fb.png)

`A small contribution graph on another GitHub account`

### The Courage to Climb

Algorithm problems felt like tall, rugged mountains looming before a beginner hiker. When I first started solving them, I kept getting flashbacks to high school, secretly wiping away tears during math class. Just understanding the problems drained so much time and energy. I kept losing confidence. I knew I couldn't go on like that. So I dove straight into the easiest problems I could find. I started from [Step 1 of Baekjoon's step-by-step problem sets](https://www.acmicpc.net/step). Even the lower-level problems often ate up a lot of my time. Still, I chipped away at them one by one. When I was truly stuck, I Googled it. To keep despair from taking hold, I gave myself a one-hour limit — if I couldn't solve it by then, I'd look up the solution right away. Gradually, more and more problems started feeling like "just a little more and I'll get it..."

![My record of solving mostly easy problems](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67324487/210123129-3d84b1b0-6fc7-40e6-ac39-ee9001d6e7ec.png)
`My record of solving mostly easy problems`

### Leveling Up with a Study Group

Around that time, I stumbled upon a post and joined a coding test study group run by Programmers. Thanks to the study group, I could reference the coach's code and push myself to tackle harder problems, even though they scared me. I also started adding comments to my solutions so others could understand them, and that process of reviewing and organizing my own code really helped me improve. I've already written a [blog post](https://nvrtmd.github.io/%ED%94%84%EB%A1%9C%EA%B7%B8%EB%9E%98%EB%A8%B8%EC%8A%A4-%EC%95%8C%EA%B3%A0%EB%A6%AC%EC%A6%98-%EC%8A%A4%ED%84%B0%EB%94%94-%EC%B0%B8%EC%97%AC-%ED%9B%84%EA%B8%B0/) about that experience and posted my solutions too. (This is why making a habit of documenting things saves you trouble later.)

### Leveling Up to Beginner-Intermediate

Coding tests are still a big mountain for me. But I think I've leveled up from a complete beginner to something like a beginner-intermediate hiker. I still need to take notes while reading problems to organize my thoughts, and solving them still takes a while. But at least now I have the confidence to step onto the trail. So for anyone out there who's in a similar place — too intimidated to even start — I'd recommend building confidence by solving easy problems first. Before tackling complex problems, think of it like getting vaccinated to build antibodies: regardless of difficulty, the experience of *solving a problem* itself builds the confidence you need to hold your ground.

## June – July: Building CS Fundamentals

### Filling in the Gaps

As a non-CS major, I knew I had gaps to fill in my CS knowledge. I took Professor Ban Hyo-kyung's [Operating Systems (2014) course](http://www.kocw.net/home/search/kemView.do?kemId=1046323&ar=pop) from Ewha Womans University on KOCW. I finished the entire course and organized my notes, though the content wasn't easy for me and I'll need to review it again. Even though I didn't fully grasp 100% of it, pushing through to the end gave me a foundation that helped me understand other materials afterward.

### Writing It Out by Hand

I bought and read the e-book version of "Everyone's Network," a book often recommended for networking beginners. I'd actually borrowed it from the library a few months earlier, but just reading with my eyes meant nothing stuck past the halfway point. So I bought the e-book and summarized it page by page — screenshotting every diagram, pasting it in, and writing my own notes underneath. (It's for personal use only, so I can't share it.)

![For personal use only](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67324487/210123219-23177df7-9010-4a5a-ae14-5de6e18b070a.png)

I even went further and drew out the data transmission and reception process step by step by hand. It took a lot of time, but once I finished, the whole structure clicked in my head.

![Hand-drawn diagram of data transmission flow](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67324487/210123220-39361007-5d3f-4ecf-a136-3c603fa988ff.jpg)
`Hand-drawn diagram of data transmission flow`

Since my school days, I've always needed to write things out and draw diagrams by hand to truly understand them. Someone once asked me, somewhat dismissively, "Why bother hand-copying what's already in the book?" They were a better student than me, so I understood why they'd ask. I remember answering, "I wish I could understand and memorize everything without doing this too." Now I'm actually glad I work this way. I get to keep records in my own words that I can revisit anytime.

## October – November: Putting Down Roots

The title of this retrospective is "Planting My Feet on the Ground" because this was the year I could finally start filling the chronic sense of inadequacy I'd been carrying. I spent the previous year incredibly busy with various projects, but there was never enough time or opportunity to study the fundamentals.

![A junior developer lacking fundamentals === yuza](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67324487/210123269-58c71a6f-8658-4336-99cc-fc03a91a73ab.png)

### Aiming for a Soft Landing

Looking back at my development journey, I practically skipped JavaScript — the very foundation of frontend development — and jumped straight into React, then got thrown right into projects. That's why my skills always felt like they were floating unsteadily somewhere in mid-air. To stop wobbling, I needed to plant my feet on solid ground and put down roots. So when I first started studying Node.js, I went back and quickly reviewed HTML and CSS, revisited JavaScript, and then moved on to Node.js. That alone wasn't enough, of course. I watched Coding Angma's [intermediate JavaScript course](https://youtu.be/4_WLS9Lj6n4) multiple times, organized the content, and dug deeper into parts I didn't fully understand. Finally sitting down and working through it all was such a relief. The anxiety that had been weighing on me eased up a little. It felt like I'd finally landed and started putting down small roots. It was time-consuming, sure, but far from tedious — my only thought was that I should have started sooner.

![Notes organized from video lectures](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67324487/210123273-d212fe8e-fc59-421a-8a8f-4948daad7713.png)

I haven't posted these notes on my blog yet. Once I've migrated all of this year's posts to my new blog, I'll upload them at a steady pace.

### Cats and Vanilla

While studying theory, I also started implementing the [Cat Photo Search Site](https://school.programmers.co.kr/skill_check_assignments/4) from Programmers' assignment hall from scratch. They provide a skeleton codebase, but I wanted to build everything from the ground up, so I didn't use it. Since it was my very first time developing with vanilla JavaScript, I spent a lot of time referencing various resources. But filling up an empty file with my own code, creating my own cat photo search site exactly how I wanted — that was genuinely fun. And somewhat paradoxically, it made me viscerally understand *why* we use React. With JSX, a single line of code creates a div. With vanilla JavaScript, you're literally growing a tree — calling appendChild over and over, hanging elements like fruit on branches. It was hard to track which element should be appended where, and I couldn't see the structure of elements at a glance. Still, finishing it felt as satisfying as it was difficult. Here are links to the [repository](https://github.com/nvrtmd/cat-search-engine) and the [deployed project](https://cat-search-engine.netlify.app/).

![Cat photo search site](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67324487/205359886-7550c920-90c6-4b6a-ba8a-9d3c1f0fdde0.gif)

### One Step Forward

During this period, I also felt an urgent need to study the basics of React, not just JavaScript. Even thinking about it now, while you learn an enormous amount by jumping into projects and figuring things out on the fly, you absolutely need dedicated time to study theory through books or video lectures. I can't speak for other programs, but the government-funded bootcamp I attended sorely lacked that time. We learned just enough — the bare minimum to somehow get through a project — and were thrown in. It was frustrating. But that's all in the past now, and filling in those gaps is entirely up to me. I'm glad I could at least shore up my foundations now.

For React, I studied with Zerocho's [free React course (web games)](https://youtube.com/playlist?list=PLcqDmjxt30RtqbStQqk-eYMK8N-1SYIFn). It's a bit embarrassing, but until then, I genuinely believed React projects could only be started through Create React App. I'd taken the default configuration CRA provides completely for granted. Thanks to that, I'm struggling quite a bit now that I'm developing without CRA. Still, studying through these videos helped me revisit fundamentals like React's lifecycle, immutability, and re-rendering conditions. I moved past the stage of just focusing on "making it work" and started thinking things like "if I write code this way, it'll cause this problem down the road" and "for performance, I should split this out separately." I haven't uploaded all my notes to the blog yet, but here's a link to my [most recent post](https://nvrtmd.github.io/React-%EA%B8%B0%EC%B4%88-%EB%8B%A4%EC%A7%80%EA%B8%B0-React%EC%9D%98-Life-Cycle/).

## December: Branching Out Little by Little

### Stepping Outside

This was probably the most dynamic month of the year. I'm the kind of person who needs a lot of courage to attend offline meetups and events. But after studying alone for so long, I wanted to meet other developers and recharge through conversation. So I attended "Our First Steps," an offline networking event hosted by "System.out.girls." Some of the speakers were people I'd been following on Twitter, so it almost felt like seeing celebrities. Listening to their carefully prepared talks, I was struck by their passion and consistency. The experiences they shared felt somewhat distant for someone like me — a "junior trainee" who hadn't even started building a career yet. But after hearing all the talks, I wanted to become someone who could confidently present a year's worth of effort in front of that many people, just like them.

![I'm somewhere in this photo](https://user-images.githubusercontent.com/67324487/210123417-d64856e1-8312-4413-bb5e-d9b33ca1c756.jpg)

`I'm somewhere in this photo`

### Breaking Out of My Shell

At the event, I ran into a developer I'd worked on a project with the previous year. I was so happy to see them that I spontaneously suggested we grab dinner together. One thing led to another, and I ended up creating the developer Twitter account I'd been putting off — just to follow them. In hindsight, it was one of the best decisions I made. I was so used to having a private account that running a public one felt unfamiliar and nerve-wracking. But at the same time, it created an inexplicable sense of accountability that pushed me to study harder. I'm grateful to have met many wonderful people on Twitter, including speakers from "Our First Steps." Interacting with them gives me motivation and helps me stay grounded. I want to give back the same positive energy to everyone who follows my account.

### Beyond the Platform

Shortly after creating my Twitter account, I also finished building this GitHub blog that I'd started working on in early December. Honestly, customizing it to match my vision gave me quite a few headaches. But the sense of accomplishment from building it with my own hands made up for the struggle. That said... using an existing platform is also a perfectly fine choice. There's a reason so many people use them, after all.

While migrating posts from my old Velog blog to this one, I realized I'd actually been pretty diligent about blogging. There were quite a few posts I didn't bring over because I wasn't satisfied with them, and even after excluding those, the number was higher than I expected. Now that I've gone through the trouble of building my own blog, I should write even more. The fact that I'm still writing this retrospective past 4 AM is part of that effort.

### Beyond My Limits

Starting mid-December, I began a new bulletin board project to build on the full-stack project from July through September. In the previous project, I couldn't figure out how to join tables with Sequelize, so I gave up on that part and kept the user and post tables completely separate. This time, through more Googling and review, I pushed past that failure. I linked the user and post tables in a 1:N relationship so users could see their own posts.

So I'm building from index.html, one piece at a time. You wouldn't believe how much manual setup goes into displaying this one small, bare page! After spending hours wrestling with webpack — something I barely understood — those past days of just running a CRA command, waiting a minute, and jumping straight into development feel like a dream.
